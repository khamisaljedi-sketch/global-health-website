A predictive, multi-source, attack-level model to quantify and characterize the injury burden and need for reconstructive surgery in Gaza

Haravu P, Lin E, Berrada O, Chugh I, Patel A, Sahloul Z, Mokhallalati AM et al.

eClinicalMedicine. 2026;103797. doi:10.1016/j.eclinm.2026.103797

First predictive model to forecast injury burden in Gaza using granular multi-source data. Predicted 116,020 injuries (Oct 2023–May 2025), aligning with 118,014 reported by Gaza Ministry of Health. Of those, 29,000–46,000 require reconstructive surgery — over 80% due to explosions. Funded by Bass Connections grant, Duke University.

Patterns of war related trauma in Gaza during armed conflict: survey study of international healthcare workers

El-Taji O, Ali A, Alser O, Ghali A, Jomaa A, Sultan MJ, Irfan B, Jafar A, Qandil M, Jeelani A, Darwish A, Mokhallalati AM, McMonagle M, Rose V, Mamode N, Khan S, Maynard N, Nott D, Chai AS; International Medical Responders for Gaza collaborators.

BMJ. 2025.

Survey study of 78 international healthcare workers deployed to Gaza (August 2024–February 2025). Documented 23,726 trauma-related injuries and 6,960 weapon-related injuries. Most common: burns (18.3%), lower limb injuries (17.9%), upper limb injuries (14.9%). Explosive injuries: 66.6% of weapon-related trauma. Provides actionable insights to tailor humanitarian response.

Cheran A, Gnaedinger A, Tolany A, Obi E, Tertyshnyii S, Ratnayake A, Mokhallalati AM, Eyayaw Mitiku H, Jewell T, Leboa C, Amoroso P, Van Coller A, Wild HB.

Military Medicine. Published online 15 Sep 2025. doi:10.1093/milmed/usaf400

Scoping review (PRISMA-ScR). Searched 7,365 records; 16 eligible reports included. All highlighted complex multidimensional injury patterns: pulmonary barotrauma, full-thickness burns, chemical inhalation injuries, and tertiary injuries from confined-space detonations. Evaluation of Clinicopathological and Risk Factors for Nonmalignant H. Pylori Associated Gastroduodenal Disorders in Iraqi Patients

Mokhallalati AM et al.

PMC4877902. Published 2016.

Study examining risk factors for H. pylori infection in Iraqi patients. Gastroduodenal biopsies examined by rapid urease test and Gram staining. CagA cytotoxin detected by in situ hybridization. Key findings: smoking, NSAID use, animal contact, and low education level increase H. pylori risk. Weight loss and heartburn identified as cardinal signs.
