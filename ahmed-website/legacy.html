My father

Dr. Mofeed El Mokhallalati

A Legacy of Vision, Dedication, and Medical Resistance

My father was a surgeon. He was also, in the most literal sense, a builder. He built hospitals. He built training programmes. He built medical schools. He built the careers of hundreds of doctors who went on to work across Palestine and beyond.

He was born in Gaza in 1955 and trained at Cairo University, then completed his FRCS in Ireland in 1989. He could have stayed in Europe. He chose to go home. That choice defined the rest of his life.

He worked first at Al-Makassed Hospital in Jerusalem, then across Gaza — establishing the general surgery training programme, building relationships with the Palestinian and Jordanian health authorities that eventually led to the Palestinian Board of Medical Specializations, which today spans eleven recognised specialties.

He founded the first specialised radiology centre in Gaza — bringing in the territory's first CT scanner in the early 1990s. He was the founding Chairman of Surgery at the European Gaza Hospital. He founded Dar Al Salam NGO Hospital. He worked, for years, to make medical schools in Gaza a reality — and he lived to see them open at Al-Azhar University and the Islamic University of Gaza.

He covered the hospital costs of patients who couldn't pay. This wasn't occasional — it was part of how he practised. He was known for it.

He called what he did "medical resistance." Not in a political sense — in a practical one. He meant that choosing to stay, to train, to build institutions, to keep the lights on in a health system under enormous pressure — that was itself a form of defiance against the idea that his people didn't deserve good medicine.

He was appointed Minister of Health toward the end of his career. He died having built more than most people manage in several lifetimes.

I think about him when I'm working in Gaza. Not because I'm trying to do what he did — I'm not. But because he showed me, clearly and concretely, what it looks like to take medicine seriously as a vocation. That's stayed with me.
